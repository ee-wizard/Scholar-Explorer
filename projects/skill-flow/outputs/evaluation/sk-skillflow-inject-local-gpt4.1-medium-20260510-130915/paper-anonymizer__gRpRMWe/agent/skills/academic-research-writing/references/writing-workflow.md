# Paper Writing Workflow

A 6-step process for writing publication-quality research papers.

---

## Overview

```
Step 1: Understand Topic & Scope
         |
Step 2: Create Detailed Outline
         |
Step 3: Draft Core Sections (Methodology first)
         |
Step 4: Write Supporting Sections
         |
Step 5: Add Citations & References
         |
Step 6: Review & Polish
```

---

## Step 1: Understanding the Research Topic

Before writing, clarify:

**Scope Questions:**
- What is the main research question or contribution?
- What is the target audience (conference, journal, general academic)?
- What is the desired length (page count or word count)?
- Are there specific sections required?
- What formatting standard: IEEE or ACM?

**Context Gathering:**
- Review any provided research materials, data, or references
- Understand the domain and technical background
- Identify key related work to reference

---

## Step 2: Create Detailed Outline

Develop section-by-section outline before drafting:

```
1. Title and Abstract
   - Main contribution in title
   - Abstract: purpose, methods, results, conclusions (150-250 words)

2. Introduction
   - Real-world motivation
   - Problem statement and gap
   - Contributions (3-5 bullet points)
   - Paper organization

3. Related Work / Background
   - Group by theme or approach
   - Explicit comparison with your work
   - Gap positioning

4. Methodology / Approach
   - Proposed method/system description
   - Architecture diagrams
   - Algorithms and procedures
   - Design rationale

5. Implementation (if applicable)
   - Technical details
   - Tools and technologies
   - Challenges solved

6. Evaluation / Results
   - Experimental setup
   - Datasets and metrics
   - Results tables/figures
   - Analysis

7. Discussion
   - Implications
   - Limitations
   - Lessons learned

8. Conclusion
   - Summary of contributions
   - Future directions

9. References
   - 15-20+ references minimum
   - Mix of recent and foundational
```

---

## Step 3: Draft Core Sections

**Recommended drafting order:**

1. **Methodology first** - This is your core contribution
   - Describe the approach in detail
   - Include algorithms, architectures, designs
   - Explain design decisions and rationale

2. **Results next** - Now that approach is clear
   - Present experimental setup
   - Show data in tables/figures
   - Include statistical analysis

---

## Step 4: Write Supporting Sections

3. **Introduction** - Now that contribution is clear
   - Build from general to specific (inverted pyramid)
   - End with clear contributions and roadmap

4. **Related Work** - Position your contribution
   - Group by theme
   - Compare explicitly: "Unlike [X] which focuses on Y, our approach..."
   - Identify gaps you fill

5. **Discussion** - Interpret your results
   - What do results mean?
   - How do they compare to prior work?
   - What are limitations?

6. **Conclusion** - Wrap up
   - Summarize contributions (not results)
   - State significance
   - Outline future work

7. **Abstract last** - Summary of complete paper
   - First sentence: broad context
   - Second/third: specific problem
   - Middle: approach and methodology
   - End: key results and contributions

---

## Step 5: Add Citations & References

**In-text citation guidelines:**
- Reference related work throughout
- Support claims with evidence from literature
- Use numbered citations: [1], [2, 3], [5-7]

**Reference list requirements:**
- Minimum 15-20 references for a full paper
- Mix of recent work (last 5 years) and foundational papers
- Include DOI when available
- Consistent formatting (IEEE or ACM style)

---

## Step 6: Review & Polish

**Final review checklist:**
- [ ] Logical flow between sections
- [ ] Consistency in terminology
- [ ] All figures/tables referenced in text
- [ ] Abstract matches content
- [ ] All citations complete
- [ ] Formatting compliance (see ieee-formatting.md or acm-formatting.md)
- [ ] Acronyms defined on first use
- [ ] No vague claims without evidence

---

## Academic Writing Style

**Tone and Voice:**
- Formal, objective, precise language
- Third-person perspective (avoid "I")
- Present tense for established facts
- Past tense for specific studies
- Clear, direct statements

**Technical Precision:**
- Define all acronyms: "Context-Aware Systems (C-AS)"
- Use domain-specific terminology correctly
- Quantify claims with specific metrics
- Avoid vague terms without data ("very", "many", "significant")

**Argumentation:**
- State claims clearly, then support with evidence
- Logical progression: motivation -> problem -> solution -> validation
- Compare and contrast with related work
- Address limitations and counterarguments
